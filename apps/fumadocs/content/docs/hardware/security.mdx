The hardware security posture is honest about its boundary: the design is **safe on a LAN or tailnet today**, and there is a clear, short list of things to add before exposing any of it to the open internet. This page is that list, plus the mechanisms already in place.

## Device tokens

Every device authenticates with a per-device `device_token`, issued at [pairing](/docs/hardware/pairing) and used as the Bearer on the WebSocket upgrade.

- **Per-device and revocable.** Each device is its own registry row with its own token. Revoke a lost device with `DELETE /api/hardware/devices/:id`, which kills only that device.
- **Hashed at rest on the node.** The node stores the token SHA-256 hashed, not in the clear.
- **Secure-store on the phone.** The mobile app keeps the token in `expo-secure-store`.
- **Enforced on every connect.** The WebSocket upgrade rejects unregistered or mismatched device tokens on every bind (loopback is the only exception, matching Core's existing behavior).

## The pairing exposure

The pair endpoint is the one soft spot. `POST /api/hardware/pair` trusts `device_id` entropy plus the `pairing_nonce`; it has no proof that the caller actually controls the device.

- On a LAN or tailnet, the endpoint is not publicly reachable, so this is safe.
- Before internet-exposing the pair endpoint, add **out-of-band proof of possession**: a code the user reads off the device (or the app generates) and confirms in-app, so a remote attacker cannot win a race-to-pair against a legitimately booting device.

<Callout type="warn">
  Do not expose the pair endpoint to the internet without out-of-band verification. The nonce alone does not prove device control.
</Callout>

## Never expose Core unauthenticated

The device security model rests on Core's own posture.

- Any non-loopback bind **requires** `RYU_TOKEN`. Core fails closed without it, so there is no accidental open node.
- Prefer **Tailscale** over opening a public port. The node is reachable on the tailnet; devices and the relay phone join the same tailnet.
- Loopback skips auth by design, which is why a USB-tethered dev test works without a token but a real deployment must bind with one.

See [Deployment](/docs/hardware/deployment) for the exact `RYU_BIND` and `RYU_TOKEN` invocation.

## The OTA gap

There is **no over-the-air firmware update in v1**. Reflash over USB for now. Before fielding devices you cannot physically reach, plan an `esp_https_ota` channel, signed and pinned to your node, so a field device can be patched without a cable.

## Ambient data and privacy

Always-on capture records bystanders, which is a privacy and legal surface, not just an engineering one.

- **Consent and opt-in.** Make ambient capture opt-in and extend the existing meeting-consent UX rather than adding a separate, weaker path.
- **Minimize at the edge.** On-device VAD means silence is never transmitted or stored, so the recording surface is only actual speech.
- **Retention limits.** Set retention on ambient sessions; do not keep raw audio indefinitely.
- **Hard mute.** Give the device a hard mute the user can trust to stop capture immediately.

See [Ambient capture](/docs/hardware/ambient-capture) for how the VAD and meetings pipeline already minimize what leaves the device.

## Monitoring

`GET /api/hardware/devices` reports last-seen, online status, and battery from device `telemetry`, so you can spot a device that went dark or one you do not recognize.

## Before internet exposure: the checklist

The LAN/tailnet design becomes internet-ready when you add:

<Steps>
  <Step>
    Out-of-band proof of possession on the pair endpoint (a user-confirmed code).
  </Step>
  <Step>
    A signed OTA channel (`esp_https_ota`) so field devices can be patched.
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Application-layer encryption of the BLE relay tunnel, beyond BLE's own pairing (a v2 item).
  </Step>
  <Step>
    Confirmed `RYU_TOKEN` on every bind and Tailscale (or equivalent private networking) in front of the node, never a raw public port.
  </Step>
</Steps>
